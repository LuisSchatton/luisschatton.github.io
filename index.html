Checkmate
In this university project, the assignment was to take an everyday object and take it out of its natural environment and bring it to life. I decided to bring a chessboard to life. In this scene I wanted to show the last, dramatic moment in the fight that an alive chessboard would contain. It’s the last move, ending the game. In the blockout I played around with scale and the camera positions. It was also important to get a feeling for how the scene should be constructed,
to allow a good readability of the scene and also a dramaturgy that intensifies the moment. The consclusion of this, was that the scene setting elements (f. ex. the clock) should be placed on the right in line with european reading conventions. Then from left to right you can see the acting piece piercing the passive piece which then results in the splatter of blood coming from the back of the piece.
All of which was to intensify the moment and bring the struggle to life. All the different armor and set pieces were modeled and designed by myself. My utmost gratitude goes out to the people working at Mixamo, for providing the rigged mannequin I used for the figurines. Same goes for the blood splatter, which I got from Sketchfab (sadly I couldn’t find the name of the author anymore).
The armor was almost exclusively built by using common boxmodeling techniques. The Chain mail was made using the at the time completely new geometry nodes (the whole project was made in Blender 2.93). The lighting setup is also quite simple, which really emphasizes the textures on the models. It consists just of a hero light and a supporting ambient light.
Bapehead
In this wonderful Workshop lead by Raphaël de Courville and Elena Falomo, we wanted to create some interesting experiences with the help of SparkAR. Since I’m really interested in Digital and Analog Clothing, I decided to enhance one of my favourite crewnecks with the help of augmented reality.
The item itself, a crewneck from Bape’s Spring/Summer 2020 Collection featuring a print reminiscent of Neo Tokyo from one of my favourite movies akira, features a badge on one of the sleeves containing an QR Code. Sadly the code doesn't lead to anything and since I got asked so many times, if the code is scanable, I decided to take matters into my own hands and just do it myself.
The process presented by Raphaël and Elena was fascinating, since it combined digital and analog prototyping tools, which made it both fast and fun to try out different solutions. In the end I landed on a concept, also inspired by Akira. The helmet should be reminiscent of a motorcycle helmet, a nod to the infamous chase scenes in the movie and the metal arm was a reference to Tetsuo’s infamous transformation. To fit with Bapes theme, bot were made to look ape-like, so the helmet got some metal ears and the arm was made to look like it came from a cyborg gorilla.
Sadly the latter had to be scrapped, due to the limits of Spark AR at the time, which only supported 2 Dimensional overlays on the body. After modeling, I added it to Spark AR and added two little animations, which you can switch while viewing the AR Lens in Instagram. both of these are holograms, glitching in front of the helmet. I also added an HDRI to simulate the Helmet being actually in Neo Tokyo.

Ryo Fukui - Early Summer 1976
In this course we used a sytematic approach in combination with different analysis techniques to translate a musical sequence into a semantic system. We chose the free Jazz track ‘Early Summer’ by Ryo Fukui to visualize. It’s a piece of free Jazz, which transports the listener into another world. To reflect this, we build a system, in which the viewer is also able to dive into the piece themselves. This lets the viewer analyze and explore themselves, transforming it into an interactive experience.
To achieve this, we used different Javascript techniques. The background for example is run by it’s own audiovisualisation script, transforming its color to the beat of the drums. The spheres are all run by audiovisualisation capabilities of Blender and then brought into three.js, which allows them to run interactively in the browser. There, you orbit around the piece as it plays or even stop at interesting points in the song, allowing you to see the movement frozen in time.
Through this programmatic approach to creating this visualization, it would even be possible to scale the system up to different songs. This makes it a tool, which can be applied to the analysis of a wide variety of different songs, even in other genres.
